Location

Barcelona, Spain

Network Analysis, Traffic Engineering

Project Overview

This project investigates urban traffic networks through the lens of graph theory and percolation analysis. By examining how traffic congestion affects network connectivity, we provide insights into the macroscopic characteristics of urban mobility systems.

The research focuses on analyzing "how much a network suffers from congestion" — measuring how efficiently drivers can travel through a network while avoiding congested areas. We utilize network percolation concepts to evaluate traffic network functionality, examining how rising traffic demand leads to congestion that disconnects sections of the network.

Figure 1: Map of the studied area in Barcelona, Spain.

Methodology

Our approach constructs a functional traffic network based on percolation theory. Rather than focusing solely on structural topology, we consider roads as functionally linked only when their speed exceeds a specified threshold.

Functional Network Construction

Roads are classified into distinct states based on their speed. A road is considered functional only if its velocity meets a given demand threshold q ∈ [0,1]. We normalize measured speed data against a standard maximal value to obtain relative speeds, then compare these with the threshold to determine functionality.

Critical Threshold Analysis

As the threshold q increases from 0 to 1, the functional network becomes increasingly fragmented (traffic percolation). We identify qc(t) as the critical threshold where the giant component breaks down into disconnected local traffic clusters.

Key Findings

Our analysis reveals important patterns in traffic network behavior:

  • The critical threshold qc indicates network robustness against congestion
  • The critical percolation exponent τ captures the network topology type
  • Connectivity analysis identifies bottlenecks that significantly impact traffic flow
  • Different time periods (normal hours vs. rush hours) show distinct percolation patterns

Conclusion

We have demonstrated a series of tools for traffic network analysis. The metrics presented indicate different characteristics of a traffic network:

  • The critical threshold qc indicates network robustness
  • The critical percolation exponent τ captures network type
  • Connectivity analysis reveals congestion formation mechanisms

Our findings provide deeper understanding of congestion formation and dissipation mechanisms. Future work will focus on controlling bottleneck traffic to mitigate city traffic congestion.
