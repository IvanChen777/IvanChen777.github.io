Urban Noise Emissions Analysis

EPFL LUTS
Feb. 2022 - Jun. 2022

Synthesis: This research analyzes urban noise emissions using three different prediction models (NMPB, IMAGINE, and CNOSSOS) applied to traffic data collected by drones in Athens, Greece. The study compares these models' effectiveness in predicting noise levels from various vehicle types, evaluates their assumptions, and examines the spatial and temporal patterns of noise emissions in urban environments.

Introduction

In the European Union, 72.5% of the population (approximately 340 million people) live in urban areas. This high concentration raises major questions about urban space organization to offer acceptable quality of life. Among the sources of discomfort perceived by city dwellers, noise is the phenomenon that causes the most discomfort after air pollution.

The impact of noise exposure on the body has been extensively studied, with observed effects including sleep disturbance, loss of alertness and concentration, increased stress, blood pressure, and heart rate. This health impact comes at a financial cost to society and also affects wildlife, causing stress and complicating communication between individuals.

Figure 1: European noise emissions directive regulating vehicle emissions

The use of drones has revolutionized data collection for noise emission prediction models. It is now possible with a fleet of drones to collect sufficient information to determine the noise emissions of vehicle fleets over defined areas for extended periods. This approach significantly reduces the required labor compared to traditional methods using multiple microphones.

Figure 2: City noise emissions monitoring using drone technology

Noise Emissions Definition

Noise emissions are loud or unpleasant sounds released into the atmosphere. The intensity of sound is measured in decibels (dB), using a logarithmic scale similar to how our ears perceive sound. A value expressed in dB(A) evaluates sound levels with A-weighting, established to account for the average sensitivity of people with normal hearing across frequency bands.

Figure 3: Decibel scale showing common sound levels

Data Preprocessing

pNEUMA Dataset

This study uses the pNEUMA dataset, a large-scale open dataset featuring trajectories of half a million vehicles collected by a swarm of drones in downtown Athens, Greece. Specifically, data collected by drone number 8 over a 15-minute period during peak traffic was used to predict vehicle noise emissions.

Data Preparation

The preprocessing involved:

  • Removing outliers (vehicles with speeds above 130 km/h)
  • Adjusting data granularity from 0.04s to 1s to optimize computation time

NMPB Model (Nouvelle Méthode de Prévision du Bruit)

French National Standard

Overview

The NMPB model, developed in France, is one of the earliest standardized methods for road traffic noise prediction in Europe. It was first published in 1996 and has been widely used for environmental noise assessment. The model takes a simplified approach to vehicle categorization and acceleration states, making it computationally efficient but potentially less precise in complex traffic scenarios.

Vehicle Categories

The NMPB model classifies vehicles into just two categories:

  • Light Vehicles (LV): Vehicles with a gross weight under 3.5 tons, including passenger cars, taxis, motorcycles, and light commercial vehicles
  • Heavy Vehicles (HV): Vehicles with a gross weight of 3.5 tons or more, including buses, trucks, and other heavy commercial vehicles

This simplified categorization makes the model easier to implement but may reduce accuracy when dealing with diverse vehicle fleets.

Acceleration States

Unlike more recent models, NMPB uses discrete acceleration states rather than continuous values:

  • Accelerating: Vehicles increasing speed (positive acceleration)
  • Steady: Vehicles maintaining constant speed (zero acceleration)
  • Decelerating: Vehicles decreasing speed (negative acceleration)

This discrete approach creates distinct clusters in noise emission patterns, as seen in the analysis results.

Noise Components

The NMPB model calculates two primary noise components:

Mathematical Formulation

The NMPB model calculates noise emissions based on two primary components:

Rolling Noise:
Lr,R(v) = AR + BR × log(v / vref)
Formula Elements:
  • Lr,R(v): Rolling noise level at speed v in decibels (dB) - represents the noise generated by tire-road interaction
  • AR: Base rolling noise coefficient in decibels (dB) - depends on vehicle category and road surface type
  • BR: Rolling noise gradient coefficient - determines how quickly rolling noise increases with speed
  • v: Vehicle speed in kilometers per hour (km/h)
  • vref: Reference speed - 70 km/h for light vehicles and 50 km/h for heavy vehicles

Key Insight: Rolling noise dominates at higher speeds (typically above 50 km/h for light vehicles). The logarithmic relationship means that doubling speed increases rolling noise by approximately BR × 3 dB.

Propulsion Noise:
Lp(v) = Lp(vref) + b × log(v / vref)
Formula Elements:
  • Lp(v): Propulsion noise level at speed v in decibels (dB) - represents engine, exhaust, and drivetrain noise
  • Lp(vref): Propulsion noise at reference speed in decibels (dB) - baseline engine noise at standard speed
  • b: Propulsion noise gradient coefficient - varies based on vehicle type and acceleration state:
    • Light vehicles: 12.5 (accelerating), 7.5 (steady), 6.5 (decelerating)
    • Heavy vehicles: 12.5 (accelerating), 7.5 (steady), 6.5 (decelerating)
  • v: Vehicle speed in kilometers per hour (km/h)
  • vref: Reference speed - 70 km/h for light vehicles and 50 km/h for heavy vehicles

Key Insight: Propulsion noise is dominant at lower speeds and during acceleration. The coefficient b is significantly higher during acceleration (12.5) compared to steady speed (7.5), reflecting the increased engine load and RPM.

Total Noise Emission:
LW = 10 × log10( 10Lr,R/10 + 10Lp/10)
Formula Elements:
  • LW: Total noise emission level in decibels (dB) - the combined noise from both rolling and propulsion sources
  • Lr,R: Rolling noise level in decibels (dB)
  • Lp: Propulsion noise level in decibels (dB)
  • 10x/10: Converts decibel values to energy values before summation

Key Insight: This logarithmic summation accounts for the fact that decibels are logarithmic units. The dominant noise source (usually rolling noise at high speeds and propulsion noise at low speeds) will contribute most to the total noise level.

Limitations

  • Only valid for speeds between 20 km/h and 130 km/h
  • Does not account for noise from stationary vehicles (assumes zero noise at zero speed)
  • Limited vehicle categorization may reduce accuracy in diverse traffic scenarios
  • Discrete acceleration states may not capture the nuanced effects of gradual acceleration changes

IMAGINE Model (Improved Methods for the Assessment of Generic Noise Emissions)

European Research Project

Overview

The IMAGINE model was developed as part of a European research project to improve noise prediction methods. It represents a significant advancement over earlier models by incorporating more detailed vehicle categorization, continuous acceleration values, and spectral analysis. The model is particularly well-suited for complex urban environments where traffic patterns vary significantly.

Vehicle Categories

IMAGINE uses a more detailed classification system with four vehicle categories:

  • Category 1: Light motor vehicles (passenger cars, vans ≤ 3.5 tons, SUVs, MPVs including trailers and caravans)
  • Category 2: Medium heavy vehicles (medium heavy vehicles, delivery vans > 3.5 tons, buses, touring cars with two axles and twin tire mounting)
  • Category 3: Heavy duty vehicles (heavy duty vehicles, touring cars, buses with three or more axles)
  • Category 4: Powered two-wheelers (mopeds, motorcycles with 2, 3 or 4 wheels)

This more granular categorization allows for more precise noise predictions across different vehicle types.

Acceleration Treatment

Unlike the NMPB model, IMAGINE treats acceleration as a continuous variable rather than discrete states. This allows for more nuanced modeling of noise emissions during gradual speed changes, which is particularly important in urban environments with frequent stops and starts.

The model applies an acceleration correction factor to the propulsion noise component, which varies based on the actual acceleration value measured or estimated for each vehicle.

Spectral Analysis

A key advancement in the IMAGINE model is its use of octave and 1/3 octave band frequencies for noise prediction, allowing for more detailed spectral analysis. This approach provides a more comprehensive understanding of the frequency composition of traffic noise, which is important for assessing human perception and potential mitigation measures.

Mathematical Formulation

The IMAGINE model uses more complex equations that incorporate acceleration directly:

Rolling Noise Component
Lr(v) = AR + BR × log10(v / vref)
Formula Elements:
  • Lr(v): Rolling noise level at speed v in decibels (dB) - represents the noise generated by tire-road interaction
  • AR: Base rolling noise coefficient in decibels (dB) - specific to each vehicle category and road surface type
  • BR: Rolling noise gradient coefficient - typically ranges from 20 to 30 depending on vehicle category
  • v: Vehicle speed in kilometers per hour (km/h)
  • vref: Reference speed - typically 70 km/h for light vehicles and 70 km/h for heavy vehicles in the IMAGINE model

Key Insight: Rolling noise increases logarithmically with speed, becoming the dominant noise source at higher speeds (typically above 40-50 km/h). The IMAGINE model calculates this component for each octave band, providing more detailed spectral information than the NMPB model.

Propulsion Noise with Acceleration
Lp(v,a) = Lp(vref) + b × log10(v / vref) + c × a
Formula Elements:
  • Lp(v,a): Propulsion noise level at speed v and acceleration a in decibels (dB) - represents engine, exhaust, and drivetrain noise
  • Lp(vref): Propulsion noise at reference speed in decibels (dB) - baseline engine noise at standard speed
  • b: Speed coefficient - determines how propulsion noise changes with speed (typically ranges from 5 to 10)
  • v: Vehicle speed in kilometers per hour (km/h)
  • vref: Reference speed - typically 70 km/h for all vehicle categories in the IMAGINE model
  • c: Acceleration coefficient - determines how propulsion noise changes with acceleration:
    • Category 1 (light vehicles): 5.0
    • Category 2 (medium heavy): 10.0
    • Category 3 (heavy duty): 10.0
    • Category 4 (two-wheelers): 15.0
  • a: Acceleration in meters per second squared (m/s²) - positive for acceleration, negative for deceleration

Key Insight: The IMAGINE model's key innovation is treating acceleration as a continuous variable with a direct linear effect on propulsion noise. This allows for more accurate modeling of noise in urban environments with frequent speed changes. The coefficient c is higher for heavier vehicles, reflecting their greater noise increase during acceleration.

Total Noise Emission
LW = 10 × log10( 10Lr/10 + 10Lp/10)
Formula Elements:
  • LW: Total noise emission level in decibels (dB) - the combined noise from both rolling and propulsion sources
  • Lr: Rolling noise level in decibels (dB) - calculated for each octave band
  • Lp: Propulsion noise level in decibels (dB) - calculated for each octave band
  • 10x/10: Converts decibel values to energy values before summation

Key Insight: The IMAGINE model calculates this total noise emission for each octave band separately, providing a detailed spectral profile of the noise. This spectral information is valuable for assessing human perception and designing noise mitigation measures.

This direct incorporation of acceleration as a continuous variable allows for more precise noise modeling in dynamic traffic conditions, especially at intersections and other areas with frequent speed changes.

Limitations

  • Requires detailed spectral data (octave bands) which may not be available in all datasets
  • More computationally intensive than simpler models
  • Assumes that stationary vehicles produce noise (engine idling), which may overestimate noise in some scenarios
  • Requires accurate acceleration measurements for best results

CNOSSOS-EU Model (Common Noise Assessment Methods in Europe)

Current EU Standard

Overview

The CNOSSOS-EU model represents the current European Union standard for noise assessment, developed to provide a harmonized methodology across all member states. Established under the Environmental Noise Directive (2002/49/EC), it builds upon previous models while introducing innovative approaches to acceleration effects based on spatial positioning relative to intersections.

Vehicle Categories

CNOSSOS-EU uses the same four vehicle categories as the IMAGINE model:

  • Category 1: Light motor vehicles
  • Category 2: Medium heavy vehicles
  • Category 3: Heavy duty vehicles
  • Category 4: Powered two-wheelers

This consistency with the IMAGINE model facilitates comparison between the two approaches and allows for easier transition between methodologies.

Distance-Based Acceleration Correction

The most distinctive feature of the CNOSSOS model is its innovative approach to acceleration effects. Rather than requiring direct acceleration measurements (which can be difficult to obtain), CNOSSOS uses the distance from intersections as a proxy for acceleration effects:

  • Vehicles near intersections are assumed to be accelerating or decelerating
  • The correction factor decreases linearly with distance from the intersection
  • Different correction factors are applied based on the type of intersection (traffic light vs. roundabout)

This spatial approach makes the model particularly suitable for urban noise mapping where detailed acceleration data may not be available.

Mathematical Formulation

The CNOSSOS model builds on the IMAGINE model's approach but introduces innovative distance-based correction factors for acceleration effects.

Base Rolling Noise Emission
LWR,i,m = aR,i,m + bR,i,m × log10(vm/vref) + \Delta LWR,road,i,m
Formula Elements:
  • LWR,i,m: Rolling noise power level in decibels (dB) for frequency band i and vehicle category m
  • aR,i,m: Rolling noise coefficient - represents the base noise level at reference speed
  • bR,i,m: Rolling noise coefficient - determines how noise increases with speed
  • vm: Vehicle speed in km/h for category m
  • vref: Reference speed (70 km/h)
  • \Delta LWR,road: Correction for road surface properties in dB

Key Insight: The rolling noise formula follows a logarithmic relationship with speed, similar to the IMAGINE model. The coefficients a and b are determined experimentally for each vehicle category and frequency band, providing spectral information that is valuable for noise mitigation planning.

Base Propulsion Noise Emission
LWP,i,m = aP,i,m + bP,i,m × (vm - vref)/vref + \Delta LWP,grad,i,m
Formula Elements:
  • LWP,i,m: Propulsion noise power level in decibels (dB) for frequency band i and vehicle category m
  • aP,i,m: Propulsion noise coefficient - represents the base noise level at reference speed
  • bP,i,m: Propulsion noise coefficient - determines how noise changes with speed
  • vm: Vehicle speed in km/h for category m
  • vref: Reference speed (70 km/h)
  • \Delta LWP,grad: Correction for road gradient in dB - accounts for increased engine load on slopes

Key Insight: Unlike rolling noise, propulsion noise follows a linear relationship with speed. This reflects the observation that engine noise increases more directly with engine load and RPM. The formula accounts for road gradient effects, which significantly impact engine noise, especially for heavy vehicles climbing slopes.

Total Noise with Acceleration Corrections
LW,i,m = 10 × log10( 10(LWR,i,m + \Delta LWR,acc,i,m)/10 + 10(LWP,i,m + \Delta LWP,acc,i,m)/10)
Formula Elements:
  • LW,i,m: Total noise power level in decibels (dB) for frequency band i and vehicle category m
  • LWR,i,m: Base rolling noise power level in decibels (dB)
  • \Delta LWR,acc: Rolling noise correction for acceleration effects in dB
  • LWP,i,m: Base propulsion noise power level in decibels (dB)
  • \Delta LWP,acc: Propulsion noise correction for acceleration effects in dB
  • 10x/10: Converts decibel values to energy values before summation

Key Insight: The CNOSSOS model's innovation is in the acceleration correction terms (\Delta LWR,acc and \Delta LWP,acc), which are based on distance from intersections rather than direct acceleration measurements. This makes the model more practical for large-scale noise mapping where detailed vehicle dynamics data may not be available.

Formula Elements:

  • max(...): Maximum function - ensures the correction is never negative
  • 100: Reference distance in meters - beyond this distance, no correction is applied

Key Insight: The rolling noise correction decreases linearly with distance from the intersection, reaching zero at 100 meters. This reflects the observation that tire-road interaction noise changes with acceleration, which is highest near intersections and decreases with distance.

Propulsion Noise Correction
ΔLWP,acc,i,m = CP,m,k × max(1 - |x|/100, 0)
Formula Elements:
  • ΔLWP,acc,i,m: Propulsion noise correction in decibels (dB) - the additional engine and drivetrain noise due to acceleration
  • CP,m,k: Propulsion noise coefficient - typically larger than the rolling noise coefficient:
    • m: Vehicle category (1-4) - heavier vehicles have higher coefficients
    • k: Intersection type (1: traffic light, 2: roundabout, 3: priority junction)
  • |x|: Absolute distance from the intersection in meters
  • max(...): Maximum function - ensures the correction is never negative
  • 100: Reference distance in meters - beyond this distance, no correction is applied

Key Insight: Propulsion noise correction is generally higher than rolling noise correction, especially for heavy vehicles. Traffic light intersections (k=1) have the highest correction factors because vehicles typically come to a complete stop and then accelerate more aggressively.

Junction Types

CNOSSOS distinguishes between different types of intersections:

  • k = 1: Traffic light controlled crossings
  • k = 2: Roundabouts

Different correction coefficients are applied based on the junction type, reflecting the different acceleration patterns typically observed at these locations.

Limitations

  • Simplified spatial approach may not capture all acceleration effects accurately
  • Does not apply acceleration correction factors for motorcycles (Category 4)
  • Requires accurate mapping of intersection locations
  • May not account for unusual traffic patterns or driver behaviors
  • 100-meter threshold may be too simplistic for complex urban environments

Data Aggregation of Road Segments

To determine the noise emission of a vehicle flow on a network connection, the instantaneous, single-vehicle sound power level needs to be converted to an equivalent sound pressure level averaged over a time period:

Equivalent Line Sound Power Level

LW,line,eq = LW,0 + 10 × log10( Q / (1000 × v))
Formula Elements:
  • LW,line,eq: Equivalent line sound power level in decibels (dB) - represents the average noise emission over time for a road segment
  • LW,0: Instantaneous sound power level of a single vehicle in decibels (dB) - calculated using one of the noise emission models (NMPB, IMAGINE, or CNOSSOS)
  • Q: Traffic flow rate in vehicles per hour - represents the number of vehicles passing through the road segment
  • v: Average vehicle speed in kilometers per hour (km/h)
  • 1000: Scaling factor to normalize the traffic flow with respect to speed

Key Insight: This formula converts point-source noise (individual vehicles) into a line-source noise (traffic flow on a road segment). The logarithmic term accounts for the temporal distribution of vehicles along the road.

The equivalent line sound power levels for different vehicles can be summed to calculate the average equivalent line sound power level across multiple vehicle types:

Average Equivalent Line Sound Power Level

LW,eq,avg = 10 × log10( 1/N × ∑i=1N 10LW,eq,i/10)
Formula Elements:
  • LW,eq,avg: Average equivalent line sound power level in decibels (dB) - the combined noise level from all vehicle categories
  • N: Number of vehicle categories being considered (e.g., light vehicles, heavy vehicles)
  • LW,eq,i: Equivalent line sound power level for vehicle category i in decibels (dB)
  • ∑: Summation operator - adds up the energy contributions from each vehicle category
  • 10x/10: Converts decibel values to energy values before summation (decibels are logarithmic units)

Key Insight: This formula uses logarithmic averaging to combine noise levels from different vehicle categories. Since decibels are logarithmic units, we must convert to energy values, average them, and then convert back to decibels.

Example: If light vehicles produce 70 dB and heavy vehicles produce 80 dB on a road segment, the combined noise level would be approximately 77 dB, not 75 dB (the arithmetic mean). This is because decibels are logarithmic units, and a 10 dB increase represents a 10-fold increase in sound energy.

Results and Analysis

The implementation of the three noise emission models on the pNEUMA dataset yielded several key insights:

Model Comparison

The three models showed significant differences in their noise emission predictions:

  • NMPB model produces the lowest noise emission estimates overall
  • CNOSSOS model generally produces the highest noise emission estimates
  • Heavy vehicles consistently produce more noise than light vehicles across all models
  • The IMAGINE model uniquely assumes that stationary vehicles produce noise (engine idling)

Key Findings from Model Analysis

Model Parameter Selection and Distribution Analysis

Since the IMAGINE and CNOSSOS models require 3rd octave band data that wasn't available in the pNEUMA dataset, the researchers calculated both maximum and minimum parameter values to establish a range of possible noise emissions:

NMPB Model Distribution

Key observations:

  • Heavy vehicles produce noise in the range of 74-83 dB
  • Light vehicles produce noise in the range of 62-78 dB
  • Three distinct clusters appear in both categories due to the discrete acceleration states (accelerating, steady, decelerating)
  • Higher speeds correlate with higher noise emissions

IMAGINE Model Distribution

Key observations:

  • Overall noise range is 73-96 dB across all vehicle types
  • High peaks appear at specific noise levels due to stationary vehicles
  • Distribution follows multiple normal distributions when stationary vehicles are excluded

CNOSSOS Model Distribution

Key observations:

  • No high peaks in the distribution unlike the IMAGINE model
  • Acceleration correction based on distance to intersections creates smoother distribution
  • Heavy and medium-heavy vehicles produce higher noise emissions than motorcycles and light vehicles

Conclusion

This research project leveraged high-resolution drone-collected vehicle trajectory data to advance urban noise emission modeling through rigorous mathematical formulation and comparative analysis. Our comprehensive evaluation of the NMPB, IMAGINE, and CNOSSOS-EU models revealed:

  • Mathematical Formulation Differences: Each model employs distinct approaches to acceleration handling—from the NMPB's discrete states to CNOSSOS-EU's innovative distance-based correction factors
  • Acceleration Treatment Impact: The continuous acceleration treatment in IMAGINE and the distance-based approach in CNOSSOS provide more nuanced noise predictions in complex urban environments
  • Spatial-Temporal Patterns: Noise emission patterns at intersections follow predictable cycles corresponding to traffic signal phases, creating identifiable acoustic signatures
  • Vehicle Category Influence: Heavy vehicles consistently generate 8-15 dB higher noise emissions than light vehicles across all models, with consistent hierarchical patterns
  • Counter-intuitive Speed-Noise Relationship: While individual faster vehicles produce higher instantaneous noise levels, slower-moving traffic contributes more to equivalent line sound power levels due to extended exposure duration

The integration of precise mathematical models with high-resolution trajectory data demonstrates significant potential for next-generation urban acoustic planning. This approach enables more accurate noise mapping, targeted mitigation strategies, and evidence-based policy development for healthier urban soundscapes.

Future Research Directions

  • Validation of model predictions against ground-truth acoustic measurements
  • Integration of building geometry and urban canyon effects into emission calculations
  • Development of hybrid models that leverage the strengths of each approach
  • Machine learning applications for real-time noise prediction based on traffic patterns
  • Exploration of noise reduction scenarios through traffic management optimization
